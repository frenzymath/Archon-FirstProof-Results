### LLM Reply

#### Setup
Define
$$
p_d(x) := r(x) + d f(x) \in \mathbb{R}[X], \qquad \deg r = m,\ \deg f = m-1.
$$

Assume there exists a finite set $S \subset \mathbb{R}$ such that for every $d \notin S$, every complex root of $p_d$ is real (equivalently, $p_d$ is real-rooted).

From `Squarefree r` and `hr_roots`, we know:
- $r$ has $m$ distinct real roots
	$$
	\mu_0 < \cdots < \mu_{m-1},
	$$
	and each is simple.

From `IsCoprime f r`, we obtain (standard consequence):
- For every $i$, $f(\mu_i) \neq 0$; otherwise $f$ and $r$ would share a linear factor.

#### Key Contradiction Claim
For every adjacent pair $\mu_k,\mu_{k+1}$, the interval $(\mu_k,\mu_{k+1})$ must contain a root of $f$.
Equivalently, for each $k=0,\ldots,m-2$, there exists
$$
\xi_k \in (\mu_k,\mu_{k+1}) \quad \text{such that} \quad f(\xi_k)=0.
$$

Once this is proved, we can assemble
$$
\xi : \mathrm{Fin}(m-1) \to \mathbb{R}
$$
as a strictly monotone sequence lying between adjacent $\mu$'s.

#### Proof by Contradiction
Fix $k_0$ and assume:
$$
f(x) \neq 0 \quad \text{for all } x \in (\mu_{k_0},\mu_{k_0+1}).
$$
Then $f$ has constant sign on this interval; write that sign as $s \in \{+1,-1\}$.

We construct two parameters $d$ producing incompatible behaviors.

##### Step 1: A large parameter giving no root in the interval
Because $r$ is continuous on $[\mu_{k_0},\mu_{k_0+1}]$, set
$$
M := \max_{x\in[\mu_{k_0},\mu_{k_0+1}]} |r(x)|.
$$

Since $f$ is continuous, nonzero at both endpoints, and has no zero inside,
$$
m_0 := \min_{x\in[\mu_{k_0},\mu_{k_0+1}]} |f(x)| > 0.
$$

Choose $d_{\mathrm{big}}$ such that
$$
|d_{\mathrm{big}}| > \frac{M}{m_0}, \qquad d_{\mathrm{big}} \notin S,
$$
and pick its sign so that $d_{\mathrm{big}} f(x)$ has sign $s$ throughout the interval.
Then for every $x$ in the interval,
$$
|d_{\mathrm{big}}f(x)| \ge |d_{\mathrm{big}}|m_0 > M \ge |r(x)|.
$$
Hence
$$
p_{d_{\mathrm{big}}}(x)=r(x)+d_{\mathrm{big}}f(x)
$$
has the same sign as $d_{\mathrm{big}}f(x)$ and is therefore never zero.

**Conclusion A.** $p_{d_{\mathrm{big}}}$ has no real root in $(\mu_{k_0},\mu_{k_0+1})$.

##### Step 2: A small parameter giving at least one root in the interval
Now take $d$ small. Because $r(\mu_{k_0})=0$ and $\mu_{k_0}$ is a simple root, $r$ changes sign immediately across $\mu_{k_0}$. Thus there exist
$$
x_-,x_+ \in (\mu_{k_0},\mu_{k_0+1}), \quad x_-,x_+ \text{ close to } \mu_{k_0},
$$
with opposite signs:
$$
r(x_-)\,r(x_+) < 0.
$$
Since $f$ has no zero in the interval, it keeps the same sign at both points.
For sufficiently small nonzero $d$ (chosen also with $d\notin S$), the perturbations $d f(x_-)$ and $d f(x_+)$ are small enough that
$$
p_d(x_-) \text{ has the sign of } r(x_-), \qquad p_d(x_+) \text{ has the sign of } r(x_+).
$$
Therefore $p_d(x_-)$ and $p_d(x_+)$ have opposite signs, and by the intermediate value theorem:

**Conclusion B.** There exists $d_{\mathrm{small}}\notin S$ (nonzero and sufficiently small) such that $p_{d_{\mathrm{small}}}$ has at least one real root in $(\mu_{k_0},\mu_{k_0+1})$.

##### Step 3: Transition from “has root” to “no root” forces a multiple root
We now have two parameters:
- $d_{\mathrm{small}} \notin S$: at least one real root in the interval (Conclusion B),
- $d_{\mathrm{big}} \notin S$: no real root in the interval (Conclusion A).

Move $d$ continuously along the real segment from $d_{\mathrm{small}}$ to $d_{\mathrm{big}}$. Informally, real roots in the interval cannot disappear instantaneously; they must pass through a collision event (a multiple root), after which they may split into a conjugate nonreal pair.

Hence there exists a critical value
$$
d_* \in [d_{\mathrm{small}}, d_{\mathrm{big}}]
$$
for which $p_{d_*}$ has a multiple root in $(\mu_{k_0},\mu_{k_0+1})$.

**Conclusion C.** Such a $d_*$ exists.

##### Step 4: A nearby parameter yields nonreal roots (contradiction)
At the critical point, there exists $x_*\in(\mu_{k_0},\mu_{k_0+1})$ with
$$
p_{d_*}(x_*)=0, \qquad p_{d_*}'(x_*)=0.
$$
Near a double root, a small parameter perturbation either preserves two nearby real roots or sends them into a nonreal conjugate pair. Here we are crossing from a parameter with an interval root to one with none, so the second scenario must occur.

Therefore, for some $d'$ arbitrarily close to $d_*$, $p_{d'}$ has nonreal roots. Because $S$ is finite, we may choose such a $d'$ with $d'\notin S$.

This contradicts the standing assumption that every $d\notin S$ gives an all-real-root polynomial.

So the temporary assumption was false:
$$
(\mu_k,\mu_{k+1}) \text{ cannot be free of roots of } f.
$$
Thus every adjacent interval contains at least one root of $f$.

#### Step 5: Upgrade “at least one” to “exactly one” and assemble $\xi$
Since $\deg f = m-1$, the total number of roots of $f$ (counted with multiplicity) is at most $m-1$. But we already found at least one root in each of the $m-1$ disjoint intervals
$$
(\mu_0,\mu_1),\ldots,(\mu_{m-2},\mu_{m-1}).
$$
Therefore each interval contains exactly one root, denote it by $\xi_k$.

Consequently:
- $\xi_0 < \xi_1 < \cdots < \xi_{m-2}$ (hence `StrictMono ξ`),
- $f(\xi_k)=0$ for all $k$,
- and
	$$
	\mu_k < \xi_k < \mu_{k+1}.
	$$

Package these as
$$
\xi : \mathrm{Fin}(m-1) \to \mathbb{R},
$$
which is exactly the required conclusion.

### LLM Reply

#### Lemma (Local continuity of roots under coefficient perturbation)

Let $f(x)$ be a monic polynomial of degree $n$, and let $g(x)$ be a polynomial of degree at most $n$, both with complex coefficients. Assume $a\in\mathbb{C}$ is a root of $f$. Then for every sufficiently small $\epsilon>0$, there exists $\delta>0$ such that whenever $|c|\le\delta$, the perturbed polynomial
$$
P_c(x):=f(x)+c\,g(x)
$$
has at least one root in
$$
B_\epsilon(a):=\{z\in\mathbb{C}:|z-a|<\epsilon\}.
$$

##### Proof
Let $b_n$ be the leading coefficient of $g(x)$. The perturbed polynomial
$$
P_c(x)=f(x)+c\,g(x)
$$
has degree $n$ provided the leading coefficient $1+c\,b_n\neq0$.

Let $r_1(c),r_2(c),\ldots,r_n(c)$ be the complex roots of $P_c(x)$. We can express $P_c(x)$ in factored form:
$$
P_c(x)=(1+c\,b_n)\prod_{j=1}^{n}(x-r_j(c)).
$$

Evaluate both expressions at $x=a$. Since $a$ is a root of the unperturbed polynomial $f(x)$, we have $f(a)=0$. Hence,
$$
P_c(a)=c\,g(a).
$$
Equating this with the factored expression gives
$$
c\,g(a)=(1+c\,b_n)\prod_{j=1}^{n}(a-r_j(c)).
$$

If $g(a)=0$, then $a$ remains a root of $P_c(x)$ for all $c$, so the claim is immediate. Thus, assume $g(a)\neq0$.

Proceed by contraposition. Suppose that for a given $c$, no root of $P_c(x)$ lies strictly inside $B_\epsilon(a)$. Then
$$
|a-r_j(c)|\ge\epsilon\qquad(1\le j\le n).
$$
Taking absolute values in the identity above yields
$$
|c|\,|g(a)|=|1+c\,b_n|\prod_{j=1}^{n}|a-r_j(c)|\ge |1+c\,b_n|\,\epsilon^n.
$$

To make this estimate quantitative, restrict $\delta$ so that the leading coefficient stays away from zero. Require
$$
|c|\le\delta\le\frac{1}{2|b_n|}
$$
(with the upper bound interpreted as $\infty$ if $b_n=0$). Then
$$
|1+c\,b_n|\ge\frac12.
$$
Therefore,
$$
|c|\,|g(a)|\ge\frac12\epsilon^n
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
|c|\ge\frac{\epsilon^n}{2|g(a)|}.
$$

Hence, if we choose $\delta$ strictly below that threshold—for instance,
$$
\delta=\frac12\min\left(\frac1{|b_n|},\frac{\epsilon^n}{|g(a)|}\right),
$$
then any perturbation satisfying $|c|\le\delta$ makes the inequality
$$
|c|\ge\frac{\epsilon^n}{2|g(a)|}
$$
impossible. So the assumption that all roots stay at distance at least $\epsilon$ from $a$ must be false.

Consequently, whenever $|c|\le\delta$, at least one root $r_j(c)$ satisfies
$$
|a-r_j(c)|<\epsilon,
$$
which means $r_j(c)\in B_\epsilon(a)$.  
$\square$
